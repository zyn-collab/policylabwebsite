Malé is one of the densest cities on Earth. We've been working on what it would take to make it more liveable – from bus routes that actually reach workplaces to community spaces where people can spend time without spending money. Below is our work so far.

Transport and mobility

Improving public transport routes in Malé City
Five proposed extensions to the Hulhumalé–Malé bus routes, from a two-minute diversion on the existing Hulhumalé route to a new third line through central Malé via Ameenee and Majeedhee Magu. Each route traced on actual roads with stop-by-stop detail and coverage analysis.

Field research · 2024
Space usage by street parking in Malé City
133 measurements across 27 streets taken at night with laser rulers. Parked vehicles block 43.5% of drivable road width. 63% of surveyed roads are too narrow for a bus. 41% are too narrow for a car to pass safely. All 27 would be accessible to buses if parking were cleared.

Elderly shuttle service for social participation
A pick-up/drop-off service connecting isolated elderly residents to community spaces. Working with Israhvehinge Naadhee to move from concept to implementation. Transport is the binding constraint on elderly social participation – not willingness, not programming, but getting there.

Third spaces and cultural life

Policy impact
Third spaces, vibrant cities
The policy framework for public spaces where people can spend time without spending money – from large community centres down to a café's back room hosting weekly trivia. Following circulation of this paper, the government announced a policy focus on youth third spaces, formalised in a Cabinet decision in November 2024.
Cabinet decision achieved
Pilot completed
Piloting community cultural programming
Free film screenings and poetry nights at a local venue on off-peak evenings with minimal promotion. Turnout ranged from six to sixteen people per event, which is the finding: people show up to free events on slow nights even without heavy promotion. Eight people watching Spirited Away together on a Monday is not a failure to fill seats. The next step is replicating across multiple venues with a consistent city-wide calendar.
Scaling in progress
A network of small venues for community arts and culture
Distributed mini-venues across the city where fifteen people watching a film counts as a normal Tuesday. Existing businesses provide space during off-peak hours. A city-wide online calendar means something is always happening somewhere.

Streets, greenery, and heat

Research
Greenery estimation in Malé City
Satellite imagery analysis of green cover across Malé and Hulhumalé. Malé proper sits at 12–16% greenery coverage. The northern residential part of Hulhumalé Phase 1 reaches 30–45% – the only area of the city near the 40% threshold where research shows meaningful cooling effects begin. Every 10 percentage points of tree cover reduces air temperature by roughly 1.4°C.

City identity and design

Draft · prepared for ADB
Consistent urban design language for the Maldives
Design guidelines rooted in the Medhuziyaaraiy / Munnaaru / Hukuru Miskiyy complex – white and blue palettes, weathered stone, Islamic geometric patterns, coconut leaf motifs. Malé's recent building development trends toward blocky single-colour panels with no connection to the city's history or environment. These guidelines propose what government buildings and public spaces could look like if they drew on what's already there.
Draft for ADB
Concept paper
Old City and Capital District
Designating the historical and government core of Malé – Islamic Centre through Sultan Park to Velaanaage and the People's Majlis – as a contiguous pedestrianised district. About 35% of streets in the area are already vehicle-free. The concept creates a symbolic centre comparable to how other capitals have identifiable government quarters, and connects to the transport proposals by routing buses to the district edge.
